About Book Consultation

Math Isn't
Taught Well.

From kindergarten through high school, math is taught as a sequence of disconnected topics. Students move through them on a fixed schedule, spending just enough time with each one to pass a test before the class moves on. After years of it, students have been introduced to what looks like hundreds of different concepts. Almost all of them are actually the same handful of ideas, and students have genuinely mastered very few of them.

The SAT isn't testing whether your student has seen the material. It's testing whether they understand it well enough to use it when a question doesn't look like what they've practiced. Years of school spent jumping from topic to topic rarely builds the depth needed to perform consistently under pressure.

I've found there are only about ten ideas at the core of the SAT math section. Students who understand some or all of these ideas find the test manageable. Students trained to apply procedures find it unpredictable and uncomfortable.

The upside is that, for students who can fully understand these ideas, the score improvement is neither small nor random. That's what I work on. When a student can see the ideas clearly enough to recognize them in an unfamiliar problem, the SAT stops being unpredictable.
